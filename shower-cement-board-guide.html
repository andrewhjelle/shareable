Shower Curb — Cement Board Install & Finish

3rd-floor master shower. Phase 4 (cement board + waterproofing) → Phase 5 (tile, grout, seal). Curb-only scope: three cement board pieces glued and sealed over the RedGarded PVC pan liner.

Stop — check this first

The Oatey #30812 X-15 in the photo is NOT for cement board. X-15 is a PVC solvent adhesive — only for bonding the pan liner to itself or to the drain flange. It will not hold cement board to wood or to RedGard.

Correct adhesive: Loctite PL Premium construction adhesive (10 oz caulk-gun tube). You should already have this on hand from Phase 4 shopping. If not, that's the one order to place before starting.

Setup — do these before starting

  1. Confirm PL Premium is on hand (not X-15). If missing, grab a tube from Home Depot before going further.
  2. Confirm the RedGard on the liner is fully cured — should be uniformly red (not pink), no tacky spots.
  3. Verify liner coverage on the outside face of the curb. Look at where the PVC wraps. If liner extends fully down the outside face → no screws anywhere. If the outside face is bare wood below the liner turn-down → screws OK on that face only.
  4. Wear PPE for cutting: N95 mask + safety glasses. Cement board dust is silica — bad to breathe.

Install sequence

The core rule: Over PVC liner → PL Premium adhesive only, full coverage, no screws. Over bare studs / wood with no liner → PL Premium + Rock-On screws @ 6–8" spacing. This is the single most important thing to keep straight during install.
  1. Dry-fit all three pieces 15 min
    Bring: 3 cement board pieces, tape measure, pencil, speed square
    Bring the three pieces to the curb and hold them in position (inside face, outside face, top cap).
    • Confirm each fits with a 1/8" expansion gap at wall edges and between pieces.
    • Plan the top cap to overhang the outside face by 1/4" — this drip edge sheds water away from the bathroom floor, not into the seam.
    • Mark any trim cuts needed with a pencil.
    • Label the back of each piece with its position so you don't confuse them once adhesive is out.
  2. Cut to size if needed 10–20 min
    Bring: utility knife or Ryobi multi-tool + cutting blade, speed square, N95, safety glasses
    Do this outdoors or in the garage — the dust is bad.
    • Straight cuts: score deeply with utility knife along a speed square (2-3 passes), then snap over an edge. Clean the back of the snap line with the knife.
    • Notches or curves: Ryobi multi-tool with carbide cutting blade. Go slow.
    • PPE required: N95, safety glasses. Silica dust = don't breathe it.
  3. Prep the substrate 5 min
    Bring: dry rag, headlamp
    • Wipe the cured RedGard surface with a clean dry rag. No dust, no debris — any grit compromises the adhesive bond.
    • Confirm RedGard is fully red (not pink). If any pink spots remain, it's not cured — stop and wait.
    • Check the liner for any nicks or scuffs you might've missed. Any concern → X-15 patch now, before it gets buried under board.
  4. Bond outside face 15 min + press-time
    Bring: PL Premium tube, caulk gun, painter's tape, drill + Rock-On screws (conditional)
    • Load PL Premium into the caulk gun. Cut the tip at 45° to a ~3/8" opening.
    • Apply a serpentine (S-shaped) zigzag bead across the entire back of the board.
    • Add a continuous bead within 1" of all four edges (this is what keeps the perimeter tight).
    • Press the piece firmly against the curb. Slide slightly to spread the adhesive, then set final position.
    • Screws — decide based on Setup step 3:
      • If liner is behind this face → NO screws. Brace with painter's tape and a weighted object for 30 min while PL grabs.
      • If the outside face is bare wood/studs with no liner → drive Rock-On serrated screws @ 6–8" spacing, keeping heads flush (not overdriven — you'll blow through cement board).
  5. Bond inside face 15 min + press-time
    Bring: PL Premium, painter's tape, something to brace with
    This face has the PVC liner behind it — no screws, ever.
    • Same serpentine + perimeter PL Premium pattern. Go a little heavier on the adhesive — coverage is your only mechanical hold.
    • Press onto the liner. Firm even pressure across the whole face.
    • Brace with painter's tape strips running to the shower floor and adjacent walls. If you have a scrap 2x4 that spans the shower opening, you can wedge it against the board for pressure. Leave for 30 min minimum.
  6. Set top cap 15 min + press-time
    Bring: PL Premium, weights (books work), level
    Top cap sits on top of both vertical pieces and has liner underneath — no screws.
    • Serpentine PL Premium across the full underside.
    • Set with the planned 1/4" overhang on the outside face. Verify with a tape measure.
    • Check with a level — you want the top slightly pitched toward the inside of the shower (1/8" over the width is plenty) so water sheds back into the shower, not out over the drip edge onto the bathroom floor.
    • Weight it (a couple heavy books work) for 30 min.
  7. Wait for adhesive to cure 24 hrs
    PL Premium reaches handling strength in about an hour but needs 24 hrs for full cure. Do not tape and mud the seams yet. Come back tomorrow.
    • Sanity-check tomorrow morning: press hard on each piece. Should feel solid, no give, no flex at edges.
    • If anything is loose, don't proceed. Pull the piece, clean off old adhesive, re-glue.
  8. Tape & mud all seams and corners 30 min · day 2
    Bring: unmodified thinset, small bucket, QEP mesh tape, margin trowel/putty knife, nitrile gloves
    • Mix a small batch of unmodified thinset — you only need maybe a cup's worth. It goes off fast (~30 min pot life).
    • Apply a thin layer of thinset over each seam and inside corner.
    • Press QEP mesh tape into the wet thinset. Center it over each seam. Cut with utility knife.
    • Skim a thin second layer of thinset over the tape, feathering the edges so it doesn't create a hump under the tile.
    • Also skim over every screw head (if you used any). Same thinset, same feathering.
    • Wait 24 hrs to fully cure before RedGard.
  9. Second full RedGard coat over cement board 1 hr application + 24–72 hr cure · day 3
    Bring: RedGard bucket, 3/8" nap roller, chip brushes (trimmed), tray, nitrile gloves
    • Stir the RedGard well (don't shake).
    • Use trimmed chip brushes for corners, seams, screw heads, and the drip-edge underside of the top cap. Push it into the joint.
    • Use the 3/8" nap roller for the broad flat faces.
    • Apply two full coats — cross-hatch pattern (first coat vertical, second coat horizontal). Second coat only after the first goes fully red.
    • Coverage is roughly 40 sq ft/gal — you'll use very little for a curb.
    • Full cure: 24–72 hrs depending on temp and humidity. Don't start tiling until it's uniformly red and feels dry to the touch.

What comes next after Phase 4

Once the second RedGard coat is fully cured, you're into Phase 5: tile → grout → seal. Full detail on the Reference tab. Order any missing Phase 5 supplies while Phase 4 dries so you don't lose momentum.

Rules of the game (memorize these)

Rule 1 — Adhesive rules
DO: Loctite PL Premium for cement board to wood, cement board to RedGard, or cement board to cement board. Full-coverage serpentine bead + perimeter bead.
DON'T: Oatey X-15 (PVC solvent only). Won't bond anything but PVC-to-PVC. It's for pan liner seams, not board install.
DON'T: Liquid Nails "for tile" or generic panel adhesive. PL Premium specifically.
Rule 2 — When you can screw, when you can't
DO: Screws OK only where the board sits on wood/studs with no waterproof liner behind. Backer-On Rock-On serrated heads, @ 6–8" spacing, heads flush.
DON'T: Any screw penetration through a face that has the PVC pan liner behind it. Below flood level (usually 3" above shower floor at minimum), no screws anywhere.
Test if unsure: peek behind or trace the liner turn-up before you drill.
Rule 3 — Unmodified vs modified thinset
DO: Unmodified thinset for seam-taping cement board (the tape bed under mesh) — the layer needs to breathe as it cures.
DO: Modified thinset is OK under tile for setting tile onto cement board (in Phase 5), but stick to unmodified for the seam-tape step.
Why: a modified thinset sandwiched between two impervious layers (board + tile thinset) can trap moisture and stay uncured indefinitely.
Rule 4 — Expansion gap
Leave 1/8" gaps at wall edges and between pieces. Do not fill those gaps with thinset. They get filled with 100% silicone caulk at the very end (after grout, in Phase 5). This is your movement joint.
Rule 5 — Water sheds outward, not into a seam
DO: Top cap overlaps the outside face (drip edge). Top cap pitches slightly toward the inside of the shower so water flows back into the drain path.
DON'T: Butt the top cap flush against the outside face. That creates a horizontal seam at the drip line — water sits in it and wicks straight through grout.

Corner & seam treatment — the "how" behind Phase 4

Inside corners (curb-to-curb, curb-to-wall)

Inside corners are the #1 leak spot in shower tile jobs. Overkill is appropriate.

Standard treatment
  1. Skim thinset into the corner.
  2. Press mesh tape folded into the corner (crease it first so it sits down cleanly).
  3. Skim thinset over the tape, feathering out 2–3 inches on each side.
  4. After the RedGard second coat: hit the corners with a third pass of RedGard using a trimmed chip brush. Belt-and-suspenders coverage.
Belt-and-suspenders (worth it here)

If the corner is at a change of plane (wall meets curb top), consider a Kerdi-Band or waterproof fabric strip embedded in the first RedGard coat before the second RedGard coat. Cheap, easy, catches the corner if grout later cracks.

Seams between board pieces

  1. Thinset in the crack.
  2. Mesh tape centered over the seam.
  3. Thinset skim over, feathered flat.
  4. RedGard covers it in the second coat.

Don't skip the thinset embed step and just slap mesh tape over a dry seam. The mesh alone has no structural bridging.

Screw heads (if you used any)

Every Rock-On screw head gets a skim of thinset to bring it flush with the board face, then gets covered by RedGard. Any raised screw head telegraphs through the tile job.

Phase 5 preview — tile, grout, seal

Full detail once you're past Phase 4, but here's the flow so you can order supplies:

  1. Cut curb tiles Phase 5
    Wet tile saw in the garage. See shower-tile-cutting-guide.md in the project folder for the full setup. Three pieces likely: inside face, outside face, top cap. Consider a mitered edge on the top-cap-to-outside-face joint for a cleaner look (advanced — square-butt is fine for a first job).
  2. Set tile with modified thinset Phase 5
    Notched trowel, back-butter each tile, press with a slight twist to collapse ridges. Level the top cap slightly toward the inside of the shower.
  3. Wait 24 hrs, grout Phase 5
    Unsanded grout for grout lines under 1/8"; sanded for anything wider. Sponge off haze while it's still fresh. Wait 72 hrs before sealing.
  4. Silicone caulk at movement joints Phase 5
    100% silicone (not grout) at every plane change: curb-to-shower-floor, curb-to-wall corners, tile-to-tub-flange if applicable. Match grout color if possible.
  5. Seal grout with Miracle Sealants 511 Impregnator Phase 5
    Apply with a small brush or applicator bottle, wait 5 min, wipe residue. Two coats. Wait 72 hrs before letting water in.
  6. Water test Phase 5
    Run the shower for 20 min. Check the 2nd-floor ceiling below. Then a week later, check again. If no drip → job done.

What "done" looks like at each phase

Phase 4 done when…

  • All three cement board pieces bonded, no flex when pressed.
  • All seams and corners taped & mudded, feathered flat.
  • Two full coats of RedGard on the board, uniformly red, fully cured.
  • 1/8" expansion gaps clear (unfilled) at wall edges and bottom.

Phase 5 done when…

  • Tile set, grouted, sealed.
  • Silicone at all movement joints (bottom + corners).
  • Water test passes (20-min shower + 1-week ceiling check).
