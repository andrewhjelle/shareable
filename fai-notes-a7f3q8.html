Our family's experience with the Food Allergy Institute (Long Beach)

A personal experience note we share with other families thinking about the program. Everything here is our own experience — not medical advice. Every child is different; the Institute will assess your specifics.

Our story (short version)

Our son had his first allergic reaction around 6 months old. Testing showed a severe multi-allergy profile: peanut (very high IgE, ~100), tree nuts (moderate), and dairy (~12). We did yearly testing for years. The tree-nut and dairy IgE numbers dropped and then leveled off; his peanut IgE stayed stubbornly high — it had plateaued. We looked at oral immunotherapy (OIT drops) but that path mostly prevents reactions rather than removing the allergy (similar limitation to Xolair in our understanding). We wanted an actual fix.

We found out about the Tolerance Induction Program (TIP) through a parent forum. The stated goal — real food tolerance, not just reaction management — is what pulled us in. We've been in the program at the Food Allergy Institute in Long Beach, CA for about a year and a half. (They've since opened a second location in New Jersey, so depending on where you are, you may have a choice.)

Where we are today: dairy allergy is completely gone. Tree nuts are going away — he's about halfway through, eating teaspoons of pistachios, hazelnuts, and similar every morning as part of his daily "doses." Peanut is the open question: his peanut IgE has plateaued, so whether he'll eventually tolerate peanuts is still to be determined. He has never had an allergic reaction during treatment.

What TIP is, and how it differs from OIT

TIP (Tolerance Induction Program) aims for actual immune tolerance to allergens, not just a raised reaction threshold.

The mechanism, roughly:

  • Individualized lab-based testing builds a specific plan for your child.
  • Treatment introduces allergy-adjacent foods on a specific schedule — for example, working through stone fruits, seeds, and tree nuts. The idea is training the immune system through related food families rather than blasting it with the target allergen head-on.
  • Each morning and evening, the child eats specific "doses" of specific foods, prepped by the family at home. Doses change as the program progresses.
  • Periodic in-person visits in Long Beach to update the plan, verify progress, and clear new foods.

How it differs from standard OIT. OIT (oral immunotherapy) typically desensitizes to one allergen at a time by escalating tiny direct doses; it raises the reaction threshold but usually doesn't cure the allergy. TIP's aim is a full end-state where the child can eat all their previously allergic foods at any quantity — something traditional allergists told us "would never be possible."

The program in practice

Cadence

  • ~5 trips per year to whichever location works better for you (Long Beach or the newer New Jersey site). Each trip is a few days depending on what's being done that visit.
  • Onboarding visit → launch visit gap: about 2–3 months for us (August onboarding, November launch). Others we've talked to have seen similar.
  • Once you inquire and commit: availability varies by intake window. In our case there wasn't much delay on their end. The several months of prep time was us doing the research, decision, and scheduling.
  • Scheduling horizon: we typically book 10–12 weeks out — at the end of each visit they schedule the next one. Hard to book further because upcoming visit outcomes can change what comes next.
  • Flexibility on schedule. The Institute is flexible about weeks you have to miss — sleep-away camp, family travel, other conflicts. As long as you let them know ahead of time, they'll work around it.

Testing

  • Annual blood test: 6 vials, but the actual draw is under 15 minutes. Our son doesn't love this part — it's the biggest thing he complains about — but it's once a year and quick.
  • 24-hour skin patch test: a big sticker sits on the back for a day. Some discomfort (no active play, avoid sweating), but for us the removal was uneventful and there were no reactions.

Daily home routine

  • Specific doses of specific foods at breakfast and dinner, every day.
  • Real prep work: weekly meal-prep sessions to make batches. We've done things like baking brownies with all the various nuts and seeds in the exact required doses, so the daily doses are grab-and-go for the week.
  • The daily nut dose is small (think small jars of pistachios, pine nuts, and similar — a few teaspoons of each in the morning), but it's every day, without exception.

Environmental allergies (SLIT)

  • The program also runs an environmental allergy treatment via SLIT (sublingual immunotherapy) drops. We layered that in for our son as well and it's been genuinely useful.
  • Plan for a headache on the billing and coordination side. The clinical benefit has been real, but the SLIT piece has been the most administratively painful part of the program for us. Set expectations, keep records, and follow up.

Example: what the visit roadmap looks like

Every child's plan is individualized based on their allergy profile, so the specific foods and pace below will look different for your child. This is roughly the shape of the program, based on our son's plan, to give you a concrete sense of the arc.

Onboarding visit → labs, in-clinic testing, individualized plan build.

Launch (Visit 1) → first in-clinic food challenges, initial food-adjacent introductions (e.g., poppy seed, buckwheat flour, coconut), start SLIT (sublingual immunotherapy) drops with a 6-month build-up.

What a single home-dosing week looks like

After each visit, our son starts each new treatment food at ~2.5 mg daily, then escalates roughly weekly:

Week 1: 2.5 mg · Week 2: 5 mg · Week 3: 10 mg · Week 4: 20 mg · Week 5: 40 mg · Week 6: 60 mg · Week 7: 100 mg · Week 8: 200 mg · Week 9: real-food teaspoons (hold until next visit)

Three treatment foods run in parallel during each 9-week window, spaced 15 minutes apart during dosing.

Dosing rules that stood out to us

  • Dose in the afternoon or evening, at least 4 hours after maintenance foods.
  • 1-hour rest period after the last dose: no exercise, no showers, no sleep.
  • Never dose after 9 PM (gut motility slows overnight; higher reaction risk).
  • Skip dosing if the child has a fever, is on antibiotics for the first 24 hours, lost a tooth in the last 24 hours, had dental work, or has any cold symptoms. Call the Institute before dosing if unsure.
Total program length: ~13 visits × ~9 weeks each ≈ 2.5 years of core dosing, plus tolerance and remission stages, plus annual maintenance appointments after remission.

The commitment — time and money

Money

Out-of-pocket cost for the treatment program can range from about $5,000 to $15,000 per year depending on your insurance coverage — ~$10,000/year is a reasonable planning number. Program length is about 3 years. Trips to Long Beach (or New Jersey) add travel and hotel on top.

Insurance

Something to check ahead of time: for us, PPO coverage was more helpful than HMO for the peripheral pieces of the program. Worth confirming with your own carrier and the Institute what will and won't be covered before you commit.

Time

  • ~5 LA trips per year
  • Daily dosing at breakfast and dinner, without exception
  • Weekly meal-prep sessions to prepare doses ahead
  • A parent needs to own the operational side. This isn't something a kid can run themselves.

We think it's absolutely worth it — and it's a real commitment. Worth going in with eyes open.

Where TIP works well, and where it may not

Works well for:

  • Kids with stable, well-characterized allergies
  • Families who can commit to the daily routine
  • Kids old enough to handle the routine (see age discussion below)

Might not work as well for:

  • Kids with complex allergy profiles, especially allergies that shift frequently. We met a few families through the process where the program didn't succeed; the common thread tended to be more complex, changing allergy patterns.
  • Families who can't sustain the daily-dose routine long enough for the immune system to relearn.

Best age to start. Our take: early elementary school (roughly 6–8) is often the sweet spot. Old enough to handle the routine and the annual blood draw; young enough that the immune system still has meaningful plasticity. If your child is 3 or 4, it's worth talking to the Institute about whether to start now or wait a couple of years. Every situation is different.

What we'd tell you if you're considering it

  1. Do the research. It's a big investment (time and money) and it's worth talking to more than one family — including families whose kids had positive AND negative outcomes. That balanced picture is what helped us commit.
  2. Join the parent Facebook group. It's active with families in the program and families considering it. We lurked for a long time before asking questions. Hugely helpful for setting expectations.
  3. Assume 2–3 months from onboarding to launch. Plan travel and time-off accordingly.
  4. Check your insurance coverage early. Understand what your plan will and won't cover before you commit — worth an early conversation with your carrier and the Institute.
  5. Talk to your kid about the routine before starting. The daily dosing is the thing that will make or break your experience. If they're solid on routines, this is a much easier lift.
  6. Yes, it sounds too good to be true. We felt the same. My wife took about a year to commit. It is real. It takes work.
  7. We're happy to talk. Sharing this doc is easier than repeating the same texts, but we're open to a call if that would help.

FAQ

Do they treat multiple allergies at once, or one at a time?

Multiple at once, via the "allergy-adjacent" food-ladder approach — building through related food families rather than isolating one allergen at a time.

How long is each appointment typically?

Two-day visits are typical, with each appointment usually running ~1.5 hours or less. We schedule as early in the day as possible so the rest of the day is yours. See the trip guide tab for the shape we've settled into.

What kind of testing is involved in building the plan?

Annual blood work (~6 vials) plus specialized in-clinic testing including a 24-hour skin patch test. The Institute uses these to build a highly individualized protocol for your child.

Once you decided, how long until you actually started?

For us: onboarding visit third week of August, launch visit second week of November — about 2–3 months. Other families we've talked to are in the same ballpark. Their new-patient availability varies. Best to inquire directly.

PPO or HMO?

For us, PPO was more helpful than HMO for the peripheral pieces. Worth confirming with your own carrier and the Institute what's actually covered under your plan before you commit.

Has your child ever had a reaction while consuming something in his plan?

No. Never had a reaction during treatment. That's a strong track record given how many foods have been introduced.

Is there data on whether allergies can return after remission?

The Institute has data on this — worth asking directly during your consultation. From what we understand, the return rate is very low. You do continue with yearly maintenance appointments after remission to ensure sustained exposure keeps the tolerance in place.

What's the 24-hour patch test like?

A big sticker sits on the back for 24 hours. No active play or sweating during. Some discomfort but our son mostly forgot about it after a bit. Removal was uneventful. No reactions.

What's the daily prep really like?

Specific doses of specific foods at breakfast and dinner. Sounds simple but the "specific" part matters — precise quantities. Most families do a weekly prep session so the daily doses are ready to grab. Example: baking a batch of brownies with the week's nut and seed doses mixed in, so each morning your child gets one brownie that contains exactly the required daily dose.

What's the annual blood test like?

6 vials, under 15 minutes. It's not fun for the kid but it's brief and once a year. This is the thing our son likes least. Everything else has been fine.

Anything about the program to watch out for?

Administrative response times can be slow. The program is growing fast. The clinical side (doctors, nurses) has been excellent for us; the ops side sometimes lags. Set expectations accordingly.

SLIT billing and coordination is the biggest admin pain point. The environmental allergy drops have been clinically useful for us, but the billing side has been a real headache. Plan on staying on top of it.

Complexity of the child's allergy profile matters. Kids with allergies that shift frequently seem to have a harder time than kids with stable, well-characterized allergies.

What resources helped you decide?
  • The Institute's own materials and consultation
  • The parent-run Facebook group (large, active — ask a current family to invite you)
  • Direct conversations with a handful of current families — deliberately including families with mixed or negative outcomes, not just success stories
  • Reading up on the underlying science of tolerance vs. desensitization
What would you tell someone just starting to look at this?

It sounds too good to be true. It is a lot of work. But if you can commit to the daily routine, get on PPO, and pick a good age to start, our experience has been that the outcome is real: foods that traditional allergists said our son would never eat, he's now eating every day, with zero reactions.

After ~1.5 years of Long Beach trips, this is the shape we've landed on. Not prescriptive — just what's worked for our family. Pick what's useful.

The trip structure

Two days of visits, roughly 1.5 hours each. We stack them on Monday and Tuesday so the front and back ends of the trip stay open for family time.

SatFly in mid-day. Land at LGB early afternoon, check into the hotel, kids in the pool. Casual dinner nearby (see Food).
SunFamily day. No appointments. This is usually a bigger outing — Universal, La Jolla, Griffith, whatever the trip calls for.
MonFAI visit #1 (early AM). Then the rest of the day is ours. Often light hikes + a favorite lunch spot + hotel pool.
TueFAI visit #2 (early AM). Followed by a low-key afternoon or a second bigger outing depending on how the visit went.
WedFly home. Morning breakfast local, then LGB.
The single biggest scheduling tip: book FAI appointments as early in the day as you can. Getting them behind you by mid-morning turns treatment days into normal family days.

Flights

Airline

Southwest, direct to LGB

Long Beach Airport (LGB) is small, calm, and a 5-minute drive to the hotel — night-and-day easier than LAX with kids. Southwest is our default; nonstop out of MDW (Chicago Midway) most days.

Loyalty play

Southwest Companion Pass

The Companion Pass is the single best travel-cost lever for a family that flies Southwest 5+ times/year. One person flies free (taxes only) when the pass holder flies — once earned, it's good for the rest of that calendar year plus the following full year.

Path most families take: two Southwest credit cards with big sign-up bonuses, which get you across the ~110k-points threshold. Google "Southwest Companion Pass strategy" for the current details — the math moves.

Booking cadence

Book at the end of the previous visit

Because the next FAI visit gets scheduled at the end of the current one, we're usually booking flights 10–12 weeks out. Southwest's fare schedule opens further than that, so you can usually get whatever flight you want at that window.

Alternative

If LGB doesn't work, LAX or SNA

LGB has limited routes. If Southwest's LGB schedule doesn't line up, LAX is 30–40 min away in traffic and John Wayne (SNA) is 25 min south. LGB is worth going out of your way for; the other two are backups.

Hotel — home base

Where we usually stay

Homewood Suites by Hilton near LGB

Suite layout with a kitchen — useful for daily FAI dose prep. Free breakfast, ~5 min from LGB, ~10 min from the clinic. Large pool.

Also good

Hilton Los Alamitos

Nearby alternative we've also stayed at and liked. Easy drive to the clinic.

Points angle

Hotel spending → Companion Pass

Hilton Honors on its own didn't give us much value. The real play is running hotel stays on the right credit card so the spend counts toward Southwest's Companion Pass threshold — that's where the actual dollar savings show up.

The pattern that works for us

  • Hard things behind us fast. Early appointments, back-to-back on Mon/Tue.
  • One big outing per trip. Universal, a coastal day trip, a theme park. Not two — pool + a favorite meal is enough for the other free day.
  • One new food thing per trip. Keeps the recurring rhythm from getting stale.
  • Come home Wednesday. Gives a day between the trip and normal life.
Happy to compare notes. The hardest part of the first trip is not knowing what's going to feel long, what's going to feel tight, what to skip. If it would help to walk through a specific week — flights, hotel, mornings vs afternoons — text or email. Easier than trying to guess in a doc.
