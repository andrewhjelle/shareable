Where it breaks — walked through step by step

The single hardest piece of evidence for the marriage is the two joint federal IRS tax liens. To see why, follow the chain:

  1. Every U.S. taxpayer files under one of five statuses: Single, Married Filing Jointly (MFJ), Married Filing Separately, Head of Household, or Qualifying Widow(er).
  2. Only one of those — Married Filing Jointly — produces a single combined tax debt shared by two people. Every other status produces separate, individual tax debts.
  3. The IRS only files joint liens — i.e., a single lien naming two people as jointly liable — when the underlying tax debt itself is joint, which only happens for MFJ filers.
  4. The Internal Revenue Code (26 U.S.C. § 6013) explicitly restricts MFJ status to legally married couples. Common-law partners (where Illinois doesn’t even recognize common-law marriage), business partners, cohabitants, and family members cannot file MFJ. Doing so without an actual marriage is felony tax fraud (26 U.S.C. § 7206).
  5. Therefore: the existence of two separate joint IRS liens against Kofi Intsiful and Deborah Sue Picard, filed nine years apart (2010 and 2019), means they jointly filed MFJ returns for multiple tax years — which means they were legally married throughout that period.

So the choice is between (a) they are legally married, or (b) they have committed federal tax fraud for roughly 24 years by misrepresenting their marital status on multiple tax returns spanning two formal IRS lien proceedings. (a) is overwhelmingly more likely.
