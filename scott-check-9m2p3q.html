Part 3Published patterns for this kind of situation

These are the patterns the FBI, FTC, and anti-scam organizations describe as common in online romance scams, with citations. Provided so anyone reading can compare against what's been happening.

Common patterns and the sources that describe them

  • "Marine engineer," "oil rig worker," and "military deployed overseas" are three of the most common professions used in romance scams, because they explain why the person can't meet in person, can't video call reliably, and might have unexpected financial needs.
    Sources: FBI Romance Scams overview · Social Catfish: oil rig romance scams · FTC Consumer Advice
  • Migration off a dating platform to WhatsApp and then Telegram. Telegram is preferred by scammers because it doesn't verify identity and is outside Facebook's fraud detection. Legitimate people can use Telegram, but the fast push there is a documented pattern.
    Sources: FTC Romance Scams · AARP Romance Scam guide
  • The "villainous ex-spouse plus current financial emergency" script. A common priming pattern is: introduce a bad person from the past (ex-spouse, dishonest business partner), then reveal a current financial problem caused by that person (frozen or hacked accounts, identity theft), then eventually ask for temporary help. This sets up the request emotionally in advance.
    Sources: FBI Romance Scams · FBI IC3 romance scam reports · Social Catfish patterns list
  • A phone number country that doesn't match the claimed location. Scammers often use Google Voice, TextNow, or other VoIP numbers from a different country than they claim to live in. Legitimate people can also use VoIP numbers (many expats do), so this is a question to ask, not proof of anything.
    Sources: AARP on VoIP numbers
  • Photo composition and stickers ("For You" rose overlays, hearts, romantic captions burned into the image). These templates are used by scammers when they've lifted a real person's photo and want to make it look "sent for you." Any single photo doesn't prove much on its own.
    Sources: Social Catfish scam photo gallery
  • Deepfake / face-swap software on video calls. Face-swap technology has become widely used in scams. It fails visibly when the person turns to the side (loses profile alignment) or when a hand crosses the face (the software glitches). Anyone reading about this pattern can search "deepfake face swap side profile" for demonstrations.
    Sources: Wired on deepfake FaceTime scams · FBI on deepfakes
  • The "Scott Miller" name specifically has appeared as a recycled alias across multiple scam-alert databases (RipAndScam profile #8061, ScamWarners, ScampoliceGroup, ScamDigger), tied to different photos and different backstories over time. Same first-name/last-name combination doesn't prove anything about any specific person using it. Noted because it's a factual data point.

Part 4Things anyone can check

Standard verification steps that come up in the FTC / FBI / AARP guides. Each has a "green flag" and a "red flag" — real answers look one way, scam patterns look another. Rationale and sources for why each check matters.

Check 1 · Video call Why · deepfake detection
Set up a video call. Scheduling is fine — a real person would want to look put together anyway. During the call, ask him casually to turn his head to the side so you can see his profile, and to wave his hand near his face for a moment.

Green flag

  • Agrees, sets a time, shows up
  • Face clearly matches the photos
  • Turns head naturally, image stays sharp and consistent
  • Hand crosses the face without any glitch

Red flag

  • Repeatedly reschedules and it never happens
  • "Camera broken" or "bad signal on the ship" excuses
  • Face looks static or plastic during the call
  • Image glitches, warps, or freezes when the head turns to the side
  • Hand movement causes the face to distort or the software to snap
Rationale: Deepfake face-swap software has become a common tool in romance scams. The tech has one consistent weakness: it's trained on faces looking forward, so it struggles badly with side profiles and with objects crossing the face. This is documented across multiple security research publications. Sources: Wired on deepfake video scams, IEEE Spectrum on deepfake detection.
Check 2 · Current ship name Why · public ship tracking
Ask what ship he's currently working on. A real assigned marine engineer knows the name of their vessel.

Green flag

  • Gives a specific ship name easily
  • Ship exists and is trackable at MarineTraffic.com
  • Its current position matches where he says he is

Red flag

  • Won't share the ship's name "for security"
  • Vague answer or long pause
  • Names a ship that doesn't exist on MarineTraffic
  • Names a ship that's nowhere near where he claims
Rationale: Commercial merchant ships broadcast their identity and position via AIS (Automatic Identification System), which is a maritime safety requirement. This data is public and free to search. Real merchant marine crew names are not classified for standard commercial vessels. Source: MarineTraffic AIS explanation.
Check 3 · Certification number Why · verifiable credential
Ask for his marine engineer certification number. He would hold a Certificate of Competency issued by a national maritime authority — UK MCA, Irish DTTAS, or another country's equivalent.

Green flag

  • Provides the number without hesitation
  • Number verifies against UK MCA public verification or equivalent

Red flag

  • Deflects, gets offended, or asks "why do you need that"
  • "I don't have it with me"
  • Provides a number that doesn't verify
Rationale: A 40-year career marine engineer holds a career-defining professional credential. The UK MCA offers public verification of seafarer certificates because credential fraud in the shipping industry is common enough that verification is a standard commercial-hire practice. Source: UK MCA seafarer verification.
Check 4 · Local knowledge Why · residency test
Ask him what pubs, shops, or streets he goes to when he's home in Bantry.

Green flag

  • Names real places instantly: Ma Murphy's, The Snug, The Anchor Bar, The Bantry Arms (pubs); Wolfe Tone Square (town square); Bantry Bay; Whiddy Island ferry
  • Gives specific details or stories tied to those places

Red flag

  • Vague ("just my local") or long pause
  • Names places that don't exist in Bantry
  • Names places that exist but describes them in generic ways
Rationale: Local knowledge in a town of 3,000 people is very specific and hard to fake without preparation. Sources for verifying: Wikipedia Bantry, Google Maps Bantry.
Check 5 · The photo, run through Yandex Why · better face search
Save the photo he sent. Go to yandex.com/images, click the camera icon, upload the photo. Yandex indexes Russian and Eastern European social networks that Google doesn't, which is where a lot of stolen photos originate.

Green flag

  • No matches, or matches only on his own social media

Red flag

  • Photo appears on someone else's social media page under a different name
  • Photo appears in a scam-alert gallery
Rationale: Google's reverse image search is weak at face matching. Yandex's is the industry standard for face recognition against Russian, Ukrainian, and CIS-country social media indexes. Sources: Bellingcat guide to reverse image search (respected open-source-intelligence group).
Check 6 · Phone carrier lookup Why · VoIP vs real mobile
Go to freecarrierlookup.com in a browser, enter the number 12362331997, submit.

Green flag

  • Result shows Telus, Rogers, Bell, or another named mobile carrier in British Columbia

Red flag

  • Result shows "Google Voice," "Bandwidth.com," "TextNow," "Voice.ms," or another VoIP provider
Rationale: VoIP numbers are cheaper, easily obtained anonymously, and can be used from anywhere in the world regardless of the number's area code. Real personal mobile numbers are tied to a physical SIM and a real subscriber. Source: FTC on VoIP in scams.

Part 5Two safety notes

Never click links he sends

Regardless of anything else — never click a link he sends via WhatsApp, Telegram, Facebook Messenger, email, or text. Not to a "photo album he uploaded," not to a "shipping company website," not to a "video he wants to share," not to help him with anything. Links can install malware, steal login credentials, or open a browser session that captures financial account access.

Any photos he sends should just be visible in the message app itself, not links to external sites. If he says "click here to see photos of my ship" or anything similar, that's not how normal people share photos.

Source: FTC Romance Scams guidance, FBI Romance Scams.

Never send money

The single hardest rule in FBI and FTC romance-scam guidance: no money moves, in any form, for any reason, to someone met online who hasn't been met in person. Not a loan. Not a gift card. Not a "temporary" transfer to cover an emergency. Not to help him regain access to his own accounts. Not to fly out to meet. Not to a lawyer. Not to a bank. Not to a "friend" of his. This applies regardless of whether he turns out to be legitimate — reputable partners understand this rule and don't push against it.

Source: FBI Romance Scams, FTC Romance Scams.
