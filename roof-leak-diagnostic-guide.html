Roof / Ceiling Leak — Investigation Guide

Investigator mode: don't jump to conclusions. Form specific hypotheses. Test one at a time. Watch what the next rain actually does.

Setup — do these before starting

  1. Kill the breaker to the 2nd-floor bathroom fan/light circuit. Confirm with the wall switch.
  2. Set up the Solar Seal cure test in parallel — bead onto a scrap of cardboard, sit somewhere safe, check tomorrow morning. Fresh cures clear + firm in 24 hrs; dead product stays hazy or tacky.
  3. Review the Materials & Tools list below and place any orders you don't already have covered.

Sequential activities (~2-3 hours total, dry morning is optimal)

Ladder up to the flat top roof — investigation pass30-45 min

Bring: ladder · camera/phone · painter's tape · notepad

You can walk the roof. Take a lot of photos. Do NOT patch or apply sealant on this pass — you're diagnosing, not fixing.
  • Parapet coping — walk the perimeter. Photograph every seam where two sections of metal cap meet, and every corner. Look for gaps, lifted metal, cracked or missing sealant.
  • Directly above the corner-crack — locate where you are on the roof relative to the wall crack below. Photograph the coping and roof surface in a ~3ft radius.
  • Field membrane — walk the whole surface. Look for tears, bubbles, seams pulling apart, blisters, or worn spots. Especially at any penetration (pipes, HVAC, drains).
  • Drains / scuppers — clear or clogged? Any debris trails?
  • Ponding evidence — dark rings, discoloration, sediment lines, or debris where standing water evaporated. These are where water sits after every rain.
Balcony walk-through15 min

Bring: camera/phone · painter's tape · notepad

  • Photograph the whole balcony surface. Look for membrane damage, blisters, cracks.
  • Where the balcony surface meets any wall: is flashing running up under the siding/stucco? Any gaps or lifted flashing?
  • Find the drain/scupper. Blocked?
  • Any ponding spots — mark with painter's tape or landmark-photograph so you can check them after the next rain.
  • If the corner crack is reachable from the balcony, photograph it from above — see if it opens wider higher up and whether it connects to a coping seam.
Attic pass with headlamp15-20 min

Bring: headlamp · camera/phone · moisture meter · notepad

Dry conditions let you see old vs fresh damage.
  • Above the 2nd-floor bathroom + window area: look for wet insulation, drip trails on rafters or sheathing, staining.
  • Fresh (dark, damp) vs old (dry, chalky, stained) — matters a lot.
  • Trace the wall cavity down from the corner crack area — can you see where the crack lines up inside?
  • Bathroom fan duct: connected at both ends? Sagging? Insulated?
  • Photograph anything suspicious.
Interior fan — disassemble + clean30-45 min

Bring: screwdriver (Phillips) · shop vac w/ crevice tool · dish soap + old toothbrush · rags · nitrile gloves · camera/phone

Breaker is off. Fan is functional; the "black" from photos was probably dust/lint, not mold. Clean it up while you're in there.
  • Remove the grille — usually pinch-clips inside: squeeze the two wire clips together and the grille drops.
  • Vacuum the housing and blade with crevice tool.
  • If the fan blade unscrews from the motor shaft: pull it, wash in warm soapy water, dry thoroughly.
  • Wipe housing interior with a damp (not wet) cloth.
  • Wash grille in the sink with dish soap + old toothbrush for the louvers.
  • Photograph the model # / sticker on the motor housing or top plate — useful even if not replacing.
  • Investigate the black tape on the bulb socket — photograph close-up. If comfortable, unwrap. Look for cracked socket, damaged wiring. If socket is cracked, replace ($5 part). If underneath is fine, retape neatly.
  • Air-dry everything 30+ min before reassembly.
Exterior wall vent cap — remove, measure, photograph15 min

Bring: screwdriver (Phillips) · utility knife · tape measure · camera/phone · ladder

Vent #1 (the bathroom fan cap). 4 screws. Pry gently — if stuck with old caulk, score the perimeter with a utility knife.
  • Measure the inside diameter of the round pipe running into the wall. Confirm 4" (or 3"/6"). This is the number you need to lock in the replacement cap order.
  • Photograph the vent chamber, damper flap, and duct interior.
  • Clear any debris (nesting material, lint, broken damper parts).
  • Note the damper flap state: intact, warped, missing, stuck open?
  • Look at the perimeter behind the cap: is there flashing / tar paper / house wrap wrapped into the opening? Any visible gaps in the sheathing?
  • Reinstall the old cap loosely for now — you'll swap when new cap arrives Mon.
Interior moisture-meter pass10-15 min

Bring: moisture meter · painter's tape · notepad · camera/phone

  • Map the wet zone around the fan opening ceiling — mark high readings with painter's tape or note distance from fixture.
  • Map the wet zone around the window frame and the wall below the corner crack.
  • Compare readings. Big wet zones vs pinpoint = different failure modes.
Talk to neighbors15 min opportunistic

Bring: notepad · phone (for photos of their damage if they'll share)

Same construction, same problems. Highest-signal intel available.
  • Ask: Where do YOU leak? When did it start? What time of year / storm type?
  • What did you try? What worked, even partially? What made it worse?
  • Anyone gotten a definitive diagnosis? From whom?
  • Does the HOA own the exterior envelope? Any collective-action history?
Log answers in the Neighbor Intel section (Tab 2).

After materials arrive (Mon) — repair phase

One fix at a time so you can observe what actually stops each leak.

  1. Fix 1: Swap the wall vent cap + reseal perimeter. Solar Seal (if cure test passed), full bead around top + sides, leave bottom edge open (weep gap). Also reseal wall vent #2 while you're up there.
  2. Observe next rain. Does the fan-exhaust drip stop? If yes → vent was the problem, isolated. If no → water is coming from higher up in the wall cavity → shared upstream cause with the ceiling and window leaks.
  3. Fix 2 (if fan still leaks): parapet coping seam directly above the corner crack. Clean the seam, apply Solar Seal bead. Only after you've identified a specific seam in Step 1 of today's plan.
  4. Observe next rain.
  5. Fix 3: Window perimeter caulk (independent of the ceiling/fan issue). All 4 sides, replace failed sections only.
  6. Corner crack fill — hold on this until upstream (parapet) fixes are confirmed. Filling the crack without stopping the upstream water source can trap moisture inside the wall.

Phase 3: Escalated diagnosis — hose test + exploratory drywall cuts

If the observation phase doesn't confirm the leak source (fan cap fix doesn't stop it, or window caulk fix doesn't stop it, or the ceiling drip continues after coping seal), it's time for controlled invasive investigation. Do these in order — least invasive first.

Hose test — controlled water isolation

The goal: wet ONE section of exterior at a time, watch inside, isolate the leak path.

Setup — before you turn the water on10 min

Bring: garden hose w/ spray nozzle · paper + pencil (zone sketch) · painter's tape · camera/phone · bucket + towels (inside stations) · 2 people w/ phones or radios

  • Two people: one outside with the hose, one inside watching + photographing. Phones or radios to coordinate.
  • Sketch the exterior on paper — divide into test zones (5-10 zones total). Label them A, B, C. Each zone is one test.
  • Bucket, towels, camera at every observation point inside (bathroom, window, ceiling drip zone).
  • Painter's tape to mark exact water lines inside if anything appears — helps map water travel time.
  • Dry weather for at least 24 hrs before the test so any leak is definitively from the hose water, not lingering.
Test order — bottom to top, one zone at a time1-2 hours total
  • Start LOW, work UP. If you wet the roof first and see a leak, you don't know if the water came in at the roof or at some wall failure between the roof and the interior. Testing bottom-up isolates each layer.
  • Suggested zone order:
    • Zone 1: Below the window (sill area, wall below window)
    • Zone 2: Window frame perimeter (all 4 sides, spray each side separately)
    • Zone 3: Above the window (head flashing area)
    • Zone 4: Wall vent #1 (bathroom fan cap area)
    • Zone 5: Wall vent #2 (perimeter caulk failure area)
    • Zone 6: The corner crack itself
    • Zone 7: Wall above the corner crack (up toward parapet)
    • Zone 8: Parapet coping — from below, spraying up onto it
    • Zone 9: Balcony surface
    • Zone 10: Flat top roof and specific coping seams
  • Wet each zone for 5-10 minutes at moderate pressure (garden hose spray nozzle on "shower" or "flat" setting, NOT high pressure — you're simulating rain, not pressure-washing).
  • Wait 5-10 minutes AFTER stopping before moving to the next zone. Water can take time to travel through wall cavities.
  • If any leak appears: STOP. Note the zone, time, exact interior location. That's a positive identification for that zone.
  • If no leak appears in a zone: log it as ruled out, move up to the next zone.
Log resultsongoing
  • Zone letter · start time · duration · interior observation (dry / drip started at X min / streaming at Y min).
  • Photograph any positive result immediately.
  • You want to know which zone triggered which specific interior symptom — the goal is a 1-to-1 map.

Exploratory drywall cuts — only if hose test locates but doesn't fully explain

Why cut drywall

Two reasons: (1) see the wall cavity to visually trace the water path (rafters, sheathing, framing wet or dry?); (2) check for hidden mold. A small clean inspection cut can be patched in an afternoon.

Locate studs + wires + pipes before cutting10 min

Bring: stud finder (Franklin ProSensor 710 or similar) · pencil · headlamp

  • Stud finder — mark studs with pencil on the drywall. Most modern stud finders also detect live wires (helpful).
  • Cut BETWEEN studs, not through them. Standard framing is 16" on center — plenty of room for a 6"×6" inspection cut.
  • Kill nearby circuits at the panel if there's any doubt about wiring locations. Doubly safe.
  • Look up: if there's plumbing above the area (bathroom floor above), be extra careful.
Contain the dust BEFORE cutting15 min

Bring: 6-mil plastic sheeting · painter's tape · paper drop cloths · P100 respirator + filters · nitrile gloves · safety glasses · shop vac w/ HEPA filter bag · Tyvek coveralls (if mold-suspect)

  • 6-mil plastic sheeting — hang around the work area, tape to ceiling and floor. If cutting in a small room (bathroom), seal the doorway with a plastic sheet + zipper strip or just tape.
  • Cover floors with paper drop cloths — drywall dust ruins hardwood finishes over time.
  • Close HVAC vents in the room — you don't want fine dust going through the ducts to the rest of the house.
  • Put on PPE — N95 or KN95 minimum. If you suspect mold, upgrade to P100 respirator + safety glasses + nitrile gloves. Disposable Tyvek coveralls if you're going to be in there a while.
  • Have a shop vac with a fine dust bag/HEPA filter running near the cut to catch dust as you go.
Make the cut10 min

Bring: Ryobi ONE+ multi-tool w/ wood/drywall blade + charged battery · utility knife · pencil · straight edge/ruler

  • Draw the rectangle in pencil — 6"×6" to 10"×10" for a good look, edges parallel to studs.
  • Score the surface with a utility knife along the pencil lines first — cleaner cut, less crumbly edge.
  • Cut with the Ryobi multi-tool — set to a medium speed, hold at ~30° angle so the blade grabs, follow the scored lines. Cleaner and much less dust than a saw. If the blade catches or vibrates hard, slow down and let it work.
  • Cut only shallow initially (½" is standard drywall depth) — you don't know what's behind. If you feel unusual resistance mid-cut, STOP and check with a headlamp before pushing through.
  • Save the cutout piece — it's your patch material later.
Inspect the cavity15-30 min

Bring: headlamp · camera/phone · moisture meter · borescope · notepad · tape measure

  • Headlamp + camera + moisture meter. Photograph everything.
  • Sheathing (back wall of cavity) — wet? Dark staining? Delaminated? Fresh dark = fresh water; old dark rings = historical wet/dry cycles.
  • Framing (studs, top plate) — same check. Wet framing near a wall crack CONFIRMS the crack is a real water path.
  • Insulation — wet insulation = water is/was passing through here. Fresh vs old.
  • Mold check — visible black, green, or white fuzzy growth on wood or drywall paper (back side). Small area (<10 sq ft) can be homeowner-cleaned. Larger area needs a decision on remediation.
  • Borescope — for cavities you can't reach through the hole (up into the wall bay above, or across to the next stud bay).
  • Take measurements + notes — where the wet spot is inside the cavity tells you where the water is entering from above.
If mold is present — decide: DIY cleanup or professionalvaries

Bring: P100 respirator · nitrile gloves · safety glasses · Tyvek coveralls · shop vac w/ HEPA filter bag · RMR-86 or Concrobium · disposable rags · double contractor bags

  • Under 10 sq ft (approx. 3'×3'): homeowner-doable with proper PPE (P100 respirator, nitrile gloves, safety glasses, disposable coveralls). Method: HEPA vacuum → wet-wipe with detergent (dish soap + water is fine) → wet-wipe with an EPA-registered mold biocide (RMR-86 or Concrobium) → let dry → HEPA vacuum again.
  • 10+ sq ft or suspected wet insulation covering more than a stud bay: remediation pro. Not because you can't do it, but because containment and disposal at that scale is fussy.
  • Never use bleach on porous materials (wood, drywall paper) — it kills surface mold but leaves roots. Also lightens the color, so you think it's gone when it isn't.
  • Bag debris in double contractor bags, seal, dispose.
  • Address the water source before repairing the drywall. Otherwise it grows back.
Patch the cut1-2 hrs spread over 2-3 days

Bring: saved cutout piece · drywall tape (paper or mesh) · joint compound · putty knife · 220-grit sanding sponge · primer + matching paint

  • Small (6"×6"): patch with the cutout piece + drywall tape + joint compound. 2-3 thin coats, sand between.
  • Larger: cut back to studs, use fresh drywall piece + backer boards.
  • Prime + paint to match. If wall is EIFS-textured or has specific finish, may need matching texture spray.
  • Don't patch until you're confident the water source is fixed and the cavity is dry.

What NOT to do

  • Don't caulk on this pass. Today is investigation. Applying sealant before you know where water is entering just hides evidence.
  • Don't fill the corner crack yet. If the water source is above (parapet coping), sealing the crack traps moisture behind the wall finish and rots the sheathing.
  • Don't run the fan/light until the housing is cleaned + the taped socket is investigated.
  • Don't try three fixes at once. You lose the ability to know which one worked. One fix → one rain → observe.

Investigator's stance — read this before the hypotheses

I've already been wrong twice on this case:

  • First error: called the wall vent louvers "broken and bent open" — they were bent by hand for the photo, normally sit flat.
  • Second error: called the fan housing "moldy" from photo staining — it's dust + lint, not mold.

Both errors were the same failure mode: over-interpreting a single photo without ground-truth. Applying that lesson going forward: no hypothesis gets promoted from "possible" to "smoking gun" without a direct observation that only that mechanism explains. The visible corner crack is a strong candidate but is NOT confirmed as the cause.

The master question

One upstream cause, or three separate leaks?

FOR one shared cause
  • All three symptoms are on the same side of the house (2nd floor, near the corner-crack side).
  • Fan-exhaust and window leaks BOTH became visible in the same 7/2 storm — that storm was severe enough to unmask an existing but latent path.
  • Water in a wall cavity finds the weakest exit points, which would naturally be: (a) a ceiling fixture opening, (b) a window frame joint, (c) a ceiling drywall soft spot.
  • Neighbors in the same construction have the same problem — suggests systemic (roof, coping, EIFS design), not point failures.
AGAINST one shared cause
  • The original ceiling drip predates the other two by ~3 weeks (started 6/10). If it's the same cause, why did the fan and window not show until 7/2? Answer could be "the 7/2 storm was much more severe" — but that's an assumption.
  • The three symptoms are in different rooms (3 separate exit points). If it's one shared source, the water has to fan out through the wall cavity in ways that reach all three.
Why this matters

If it's one shared upstream cause, point-fixing the fan cap and window caulk won't stop the ceiling drip. If it's three separate causes, each fix helps only that symptom. The test in the plan (fix the fan cap first, observe) is designed to distinguish these two possibilities in a single rain cycle.

Hypotheses per leak point (with reasons for and against)

Leak 1 — Original ceiling drip (since 6/10, on/off with weather)

H1a: Failed parapet coping seam above the drip zone
ForCoping seams are #1 leak source on flat-roof buildings with parapets. On-off drip pattern matches storm intensity, consistent with wind-driven rain finding a gap.
AgainstHaven't yet located a specific coping seam directly above the drip zone. Assumption right now, not observation.
TestRoof walk (Step 1 today). Photograph every coping seam. Correlate positions with the ceiling drip location below. Look for gapped, lifted, or sealant-failed seams.
FalsifierIf all coping seams are visually intact + no failed sealant, this hypothesis weakens. If you find one obviously bad seam above the drip zone, it strengthens (but still isn't confirmed until sealing it stops the leak).
H1b: Flat-roof field membrane tear or penetration failure
ForFlat-roof membranes crack at penetrations (pipes, HVAC, drains) and at any spot with foot traffic. On-off drip pattern is consistent with rain intensity.
AgainstField membrane failures usually cause chronic leaks, not on-off. Also: you don't spend much time walking on the roof (I assume), so foot-traffic damage is unlikely.
TestRoof walk (Step 1). Look at every penetration + walk the whole membrane looking for tears, bubbles, seams pulling apart.
FalsifierClean field = eliminates this. Visible damage at a specific spot = investigate whether it lines up with the drip below.
H1c: Corner crack in wall itself (water bypasses roof, enters directly through EIFS/stucco)
ForVisible crack in a place where thermal expansion concentrates. If crack is deep enough to breach the moisture barrier behind the finish, wind-driven rain can drive water in.
AgainstSurface cracks don't automatically breach the moisture barrier. The 6/10 leak also happened during less severe rain than 7/2, which is when the crack would be MOST likely to leak. If the crack were the primary cause, you'd expect the ceiling to worsen dramatically on 7/2 (did it?). This hypothesis puts the crack as CAUSE; it might be an effect of something else (settling, or an interior wet spot pushing outward).
TestDuring the next moderate rain, watch this specific crack. Is water visibly entering it, or running past it? Also: probe depth from outside with a thin metal pin (careful) — is it hairline surface, or does it feel like it goes through?
FalsifierRain runs past without entering = eliminates this. Or: if the ceiling drip started well before the crack was there (check old photos of the exterior).
H1d: Gutter/scupper overflow directing water where it shouldn't go
ForBlocked drainage = water sheets over the edge, finds gaps in fascia, siding, or window trim.
AgainstWould explain leaks during heavy rain but not moderate. Would probably show as staining on the outside wall below the overflow point.
TestCheck gutters + scuppers for debris (Step 1). Watch during next rain.
FalsifierClean gutters + observed proper drainage during rain = eliminates.
H1e: Design/construction defect shared with the townhouse community
ForMultiple neighbors have the same problem. Contractors haven't been able to help anyone. This is a strong signal that the failure mode is designed-in, not a specific unit's damage.
Against"Shared problem" doesn't tell you the specific mechanism — could still be one of H1a-H1d, just shared across units.
TestNeighbor conversations (Step 7). What did they find? What patterns match your experience?
FalsifierNot really falsifiable as stated — but neighbor info might POINT to which specific mechanism is the shared one.

Leak 2 — Bathroom fan exhaust dripping (new 7/2)

H2a: Failed backdraft damper inside the wall vent
ForLouver caps rely on the damper flap for wind-driven rain protection. Invisible from outside. Would explain why a storm-severe event triggered a new leak.
AgainstHaven't confirmed damper state yet.
TestRemove cap in Step 5. Photograph damper. Test manually — does it swing freely, seal on the closed side, spring back?
FalsifierDamper works correctly = eliminates. Also: replacing the cap and rain-testing directly.
H2b: Failed perimeter caulking around the wall vent frame
ForVent #2 photo clearly shows this failure mode nearby. Reasonable prior that #1 has some version of the same.
AgainstDidn't clearly see it in the vent #1 photo. Different vent, could be different condition.
TestVisual inspection during Step 5 removal.
FalsifierPerimeter seal intact = eliminates.
H2c: Duct disconnected or improperly sloped inside the wall
ForCommon in older installs. If disconnected at the cap side, any rain entering the cap dumps into the wall cavity.
AgainstWould probably cause smaller chronic leaks over time, not a big new leak from one storm.
TestBorescope up the duct on Monday, both from wall side and from fan side. See if the duct is continuous and sloped correctly.
FalsifierDuct visibly continuous + sloped correctly = eliminates.
H2d: Water enters higher up (parapet, corner crack, roof) and drops out fan opening as weakest interior exit
ForWould explain why a NEW leak appeared during a severe storm — the fan opening is a soft spot in the interior finish, and wall-cavity water finds it. Consistent with the "one upstream cause" master hypothesis.
AgainstThe fan is directly above a wall vent to the outside. Simpler explanations (vent failure) are more likely by Occam's razor. But simpler ≠ correct.
TestFix the wall vent cap perfectly. Observe next rain. If fan STILL leaks, water is not coming from the vent — it's coming from higher up, and this hypothesis strengthens.
FalsifierCap fix stops the leak = eliminates. Cap fix doesn't stop the leak = strengthens (but doesn't confirm, other H2 causes could also survive the fix).

Leak 3 — 2nd-floor window (new 7/2)

H3a: Failed exterior caulk around window frame
ForCaulk has 5-10 year life, easy to check. Most common window-leak cause.
AgainstHaven't visually checked yet.
TestVisual inspection of all 4 sides of the frame from outside (ladder). Look for cracked, gapped, or missing caulk beads. Note where the water enters (top/side/bottom) after the next rain.
FalsifierCaulk intact + water still enters = not this. Failed caulk found + repaired + leak stops = confirmed.
H3b: Missing or failed head flashing / drip cap above window
ForEspecially if this is EIFS — head flashing failures are a well-known EIFS problem.
AgainstHaven't checked yet.
TestLook above the window from ladder height. Is there a metal drip cap extending out over the top of the frame?
FalsifierPresent + intact = eliminates. Missing/failed + water enters at top of frame during next rain = strong signal.
H3c: Water tracking from corner crack / parapet (shared with H1 + H2d)
ForSame argument as H2d. Wall cavity water finds the window frame as an exit.
AgainstSame as H2d. Simpler window-frame causes should be checked first.
TestFix window perimeter caulk (H3a). Observe. If still leaks → upstream.
FalsifierPerimeter fix stops the leak = eliminates.

Skeptical notes to self

  • Don't over-weight what's visible. The corner crack is the most photogenic thing in these photos. That doesn't mean it's the primary problem. Look for less-photogenic failure modes too.
  • Don't confuse correlation with causation. If sealing the vent perimeter stops the fan leak, it's more likely to be caulk failure than damper failure — but you don't know for sure because both got "fixed" simultaneously. Isolate variables when possible.
  • Storm severity as a confounder. The 7/2 storm was severe. New leaks in a severe storm might not mean "new failures" — they might mean "existing latent failures unmasked at higher water volume." Compare vs known storm intensity: was 7/2 the worst since 6/10?
  • Neighbor pattern is the strongest signal. Multiple units with the same problem strongly suggests the failure is designed-in. Ask specifically: are the leaks in the same rooms as yours? Same walls?
  • Fixes that work by accident tell you nothing. If you fix three things at once and the leak stops, you haven't diagnosed anything. One-fix-one-rain-one-observation discipline.
