The roof coating — end-of-life diagnosis + recoat plan

What you're looking at

The 4th-floor roof is a two-layer system:

  • Underlayer: the actual waterproof membrane (modified bitumen or single-ply). Does the real sealing.
  • Top layer: the white elastomeric coating. Protects the membrane from UV, provides "cool-roof" reflectivity, acts as secondary waterproofing.

Coatings have a 7-10 year life and are meant to be recoated periodically. The universal craze-cracking pattern in every photo = UV degradation. The membrane below may still be OK, but:

  • UV is now hitting the membrane directly → accelerating its aging.
  • Coating no longer flexes with temperature → cracks widen every freeze/thaw.
  • Water sits in cracks and slowly works through to the membrane.
  • Eventually the membrane starts failing in random spots unrelated to today's known leaks.

Sequential recoat activities (Option A — after point fixes are observed)

Sweep the roof clean30 min

Bring: broom · shop vac w/ crevice tool · nitrile gloves

  • Broom sweep first, then vacuum edges + around penetrations where debris collects.
  • Don't skip this — anything left on the roof gets sealed under two coats of coating and creates a bump/weakspot.
Prep every wide crack45-60 min

Bring: wire brush · putty knife · mesh tape (FibaTape or QEP) · Solar Seal · caulk gun · alcohol · rag

  • Which cracks: cracks radiating from penetrations · wide cracks with dark line underneath · pad-to-roof caulk failures (AC pad edge, photos 172607, 172700).
  • Skip: hairline craze cracks — the recoat handles these. Don't waste time spot-treating them.
  • Wire-brush the crack + a couple inches on each side to sound material.
  • Alcohol wipe, let dry.
  • Apply a bead of Solar Seal along the crack.
  • Press mesh tape (2" wide) into the wet sealant, centered over the crack.
  • Skim a second thin layer of Solar Seal over the tape, feathering out 2-3" on each side.
Reinforce every penetration45 min

Bring: reinforcing fabric (or mesh tape as backup) · Solar Seal · caulk gun · scissors · nitrile gloves

  • Every vent stack, HVAC pad edge, drain, and parapet base gets a fabric-reinforced flashing detail.
  • Cut fabric strips ~6-8" wide, long enough to wrap the perimeter of each penetration.
  • Same sandwich as the crack repair: Solar Seal → fabric pressed in → Solar Seal skim on top → feather edges.
  • This is where 90% of future leaks will come from. Overdo it here.
Apply first coat of roof coating2-3 hrs, plus 12-24 hr cure

Bring: Ames Blue Max or Henry Tropi-Cool 887 · 3/4" nap roller + frame + tray · chip brushes for detail · nitrile gloves · old clothes

  • Stir the bucket well (paddle mixer or a stick — no shaking).
  • Chip brushes for penetrations, seams, and parapet base first. Push coating into every corner.
  • Then 3/4" nap roller on the flats. Roll in one direction (say north-south for the first coat).
  • Aim for ~50 sq ft per gallon on this coat. Coating goes on pink and dries white/red depending on brand — check package for cure indicator.
  • Let cure per package. Typically 12-24 hrs before recoat, longer in humidity.
  • Dry weather forecast is required — no rain in the 24 hrs after each coat.
Apply second coat cross-hatched2-3 hrs, plus 24-72 hr cure

Bring: same as previous step, fresh roller cover

  • Roll perpendicular to the first coat direction (east-west if first was north-south). Cross-hatch pattern eliminates thin spots.
  • Same coverage rate — ~50 sq ft/gal. This coat is where the waterproofing actually happens.
  • Full cure: 24-72 hrs depending on temp/humidity. Don't walk on it for a week if you can avoid it.
Observe through 2-3 rain cyclesweeks
  • Watch every known leak location.
  • Photograph any recoated area where the coating shows any early wear (thin spots, blisters, discoloration).
  • Small touch-ups are normal in the first month — a leftover pint of coating + a chip brush.
  • Log successful weathering — you want to know how many storms it took to prove it out.

Phase 3: Escalated diagnosis — hose test + exploratory drywall cuts

If the observation phase doesn't confirm the leak source (fan cap fix doesn't stop it, or window caulk fix doesn't stop it, or the ceiling drip continues after coping seal), it's time for controlled invasive investigation. Do these in order — least invasive first.

Hose test — controlled water isolation

The goal: wet ONE section of exterior at a time, watch inside, isolate the leak path.

Setup — before you turn the water on10 min

Bring: garden hose w/ spray nozzle · paper + pencil (zone sketch) · painter's tape · camera/phone · bucket + towels (inside stations) · 2 people w/ phones or radios

  • Two people: one outside with the hose, one inside watching + photographing. Phones or radios to coordinate.
  • Sketch the exterior on paper — divide into test zones (5-10 zones total). Label them A, B, C. Each zone is one test.
  • Bucket, towels, camera at every observation point inside (bathroom, window, ceiling drip zone).
  • Painter's tape to mark exact water lines inside if anything appears — helps map water travel time.
  • Dry weather for at least 24 hrs before the test so any leak is definitively from the hose water, not lingering.
Test order — bottom to top, one zone at a time1-2 hours total
  • Start LOW, work UP. If you wet the roof first and see a leak, you don't know if the water came in at the roof or at some wall failure between the roof and the interior. Testing bottom-up isolates each layer.
  • Suggested zone order:
    • Zone 1: Below the window (sill area, wall below window)
    • Zone 2: Window frame perimeter (all 4 sides, spray each side separately)
    • Zone 3: Above the window (head flashing area)
    • Zone 4: Wall vent #1 (bathroom fan cap area)
    • Zone 5: Wall vent #2 (perimeter caulk failure area)
    • Zone 6: The corner crack itself
    • Zone 7: Wall above the corner crack (up toward parapet)
    • Zone 8: Parapet coping — from below, spraying up onto it
    • Zone 9: Balcony surface
    • Zone 10: Flat top roof and specific coping seams
  • Wet each zone for 5-10 minutes at moderate pressure (garden hose spray nozzle on "shower" or "flat" setting, NOT high pressure — you're simulating rain, not pressure-washing).
  • Wait 5-10 minutes AFTER stopping before moving to the next zone. Water can take time to travel through wall cavities.
  • If any leak appears: STOP. Note the zone, time, exact interior location. That's a positive identification for that zone.
  • If no leak appears in a zone: log it as ruled out, move up to the next zone.
Log resultsongoing
  • Zone letter · start time · duration · interior observation (dry / drip started at X min / streaming at Y min).
  • Photograph any positive result immediately.
  • You want to know which zone triggered which specific interior symptom — the goal is a 1-to-1 map.

Exploratory drywall cuts — only if hose test locates but doesn't fully explain

Why cut drywall

Two reasons: (1) see the wall cavity to visually trace the water path (rafters, sheathing, framing wet or dry?); (2) check for hidden mold. A small clean inspection cut can be patched in an afternoon.

Locate studs + wires + pipes before cutting10 min

Bring: stud finder (Franklin ProSensor 710 or similar) · pencil · headlamp

  • Stud finder — mark studs with pencil on the drywall. Most modern stud finders also detect live wires (helpful).
  • Cut BETWEEN studs, not through them. Standard framing is 16" on center — plenty of room for a 6"×6" inspection cut.
  • Kill nearby circuits at the panel if there's any doubt about wiring locations. Doubly safe.
  • Look up: if there's plumbing above the area (bathroom floor above), be extra careful.
Contain the dust BEFORE cutting15 min

Bring: 6-mil plastic sheeting · painter's tape · paper drop cloths · P100 respirator + filters · nitrile gloves · safety glasses · shop vac w/ HEPA filter bag · Tyvek coveralls (if mold-suspect)

  • 6-mil plastic sheeting — hang around the work area, tape to ceiling and floor. If cutting in a small room (bathroom), seal the doorway with a plastic sheet + zipper strip or just tape.
  • Cover floors with paper drop cloths — drywall dust ruins hardwood finishes over time.
  • Close HVAC vents in the room — you don't want fine dust going through the ducts to the rest of the house.
  • Put on PPE — N95 or KN95 minimum. If you suspect mold, upgrade to P100 respirator + safety glasses + nitrile gloves. Disposable Tyvek coveralls if you're going to be in there a while.
  • Have a shop vac with a fine dust bag/HEPA filter running near the cut to catch dust as you go.
Make the cut10 min

Bring: Ryobi ONE+ multi-tool w/ wood/drywall blade + charged battery · utility knife · pencil · straight edge/ruler

  • Draw the rectangle in pencil — 6"×6" to 10"×10" for a good look, edges parallel to studs.
  • Score the surface with a utility knife along the pencil lines first — cleaner cut, less crumbly edge.
  • Cut with the Ryobi multi-tool — set to a medium speed, hold at ~30° angle so the blade grabs, follow the scored lines. Cleaner and much less dust than a saw. If the blade catches or vibrates hard, slow down and let it work.
  • Cut only shallow initially (½" is standard drywall depth) — you don't know what's behind. If you feel unusual resistance mid-cut, STOP and check with a headlamp before pushing through.
  • Save the cutout piece — it's your patch material later.
Inspect the cavity15-30 min

Bring: headlamp · camera/phone · moisture meter · borescope · notepad · tape measure

  • Headlamp + camera + moisture meter. Photograph everything.
  • Sheathing (back wall of cavity) — wet? Dark staining? Delaminated? Fresh dark = fresh water; old dark rings = historical wet/dry cycles.
  • Framing (studs, top plate) — same check. Wet framing near a wall crack CONFIRMS the crack is a real water path.
  • Insulation — wet insulation = water is/was passing through here. Fresh vs old.
  • Mold check — visible black, green, or white fuzzy growth on wood or drywall paper (back side). Small area (<10 sq ft) can be homeowner-cleaned. Larger area needs a decision on remediation.
  • Borescope — for cavities you can't reach through the hole (up into the wall bay above, or across to the next stud bay).
  • Take measurements + notes — where the wet spot is inside the cavity tells you where the water is entering from above.
If mold is present — decide: DIY cleanup or professionalvaries

Bring: P100 respirator · nitrile gloves · safety glasses · Tyvek coveralls · shop vac w/ HEPA filter bag · RMR-86 or Concrobium · disposable rags · double contractor bags

  • Under 10 sq ft (approx. 3'×3'): homeowner-doable with proper PPE (P100 respirator, nitrile gloves, safety glasses, disposable coveralls). Method: HEPA vacuum → wet-wipe with detergent (dish soap + water is fine) → wet-wipe with an EPA-registered mold biocide (RMR-86 or Concrobium) → let dry → HEPA vacuum again.
  • 10+ sq ft or suspected wet insulation covering more than a stud bay: remediation pro. Not because you can't do it, but because containment and disposal at that scale is fussy.
  • Never use bleach on porous materials (wood, drywall paper) — it kills surface mold but leaves roots. Also lightens the color, so you think it's gone when it isn't.
  • Bag debris in double contractor bags, seal, dispose.
  • Address the water source before repairing the drywall. Otherwise it grows back.
Patch the cut1-2 hrs spread over 2-3 days

Bring: saved cutout piece · drywall tape (paper or mesh) · joint compound · putty knife · 220-grit sanding sponge · primer + matching paint

  • Small (6"×6"): patch with the cutout piece + drywall tape + joint compound. 2-3 thin coats, sand between.
  • Larger: cut back to studs, use fresh drywall piece + backer boards.
  • Prime + paint to match. If wall is EIFS-textured or has specific finish, may need matching texture spray.
  • Don't patch until you're confident the water source is fixed and the cavity is dry.

What NOT to do

  • Don't caulk on this pass. Today is investigation. Applying sealant before you know where water is entering just hides evidence.
  • Don't fill the corner crack yet. If the water source is above (parapet coping), sealing the crack traps moisture behind the wall finish and rots the sheathing.
  • Don't run the fan/light until the housing is cleaned + the taped socket is investigated.
  • Don't try three fixes at once. You lose the ability to know which one worked. One fix → one rain → observe.

The master question

One upstream cause, or three separate leaks?

FOR one shared cause
  • All three symptoms are on the same side of the house (2nd floor, near the corner-crack side).
  • Fan-exhaust and window leaks BOTH became visible in the same 7/2 storm — that storm was severe enough to unmask an existing but latent path.
  • Water in a wall cavity finds the weakest exit points, which would naturally be: (a) a ceiling fixture opening, (b) a window frame joint, (c) a ceiling drywall soft spot.
  • Neighbors in the same construction have the same problem — suggests systemic (roof, coping, EIFS design), not point failures.
AGAINST one shared cause
  • The original ceiling drip predates the other two by ~3 weeks (started 6/10). If it's the same cause, why did the fan and window not show until 7/2? Answer could be "the 7/2 storm was much more severe" — but that's an assumption.
  • The three symptoms are in different rooms (3 separate exit points). If it's one shared source, the water has to fan out through the wall cavity in ways that reach all three.
Why this matters

If it's one shared upstream cause, point-fixing the fan cap and window caulk won't stop the ceiling drip. If it's three separate causes, each fix helps only that symptom. The test in the plan (fix the fan cap first, observe) is designed to distinguish these two possibilities in a single rain cycle.

Skeptical notes to self

  • Don't over-weight what's visible. The corner crack is the most photogenic thing in these photos. That doesn't mean it's the primary problem. Look for less-photogenic failure modes too.
  • Don't confuse correlation with causation. If sealing the vent perimeter stops the fan leak, it's more likely to be caulk failure than damper failure — but you don't know for sure because both got "fixed" simultaneously. Isolate variables when possible.
  • Storm severity as a confounder. The 7/2 storm was severe. New leaks in a severe storm might not mean "new failures" — they might mean "existing latent failures unmasked at higher water volume." Compare vs known storm intensity: was 7/2 the worst since 6/10?
  • Neighbor pattern is the strongest signal. Multiple units with the same problem strongly suggests the failure is designed-in. Ask specifically: are the leaks in the same rooms as yours? Same walls?
  • Fixes that work by accident tell you nothing. If you fix three things at once and the leak stops, you haven't diagnosed anything. One-fix-one-rain-one-observation discipline.
